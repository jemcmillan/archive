Power Projection Through Information, Communication

  • Written by MCCM(SW/AW/EXW) Jon McMillan
  • with RDML Dawn Cutler
  • Delivered by RDML Cutler at the New Flag and Senior Executive Training Symposium
  • Washington D.C.
  • October 6, 2016

On screen: “There is a war out there, old friend- a World War. And it’s not about whose got the most bullets; it’s about who controls the information.” Cosmo, Ben Kingsley’s character in the 1992 Film “Sneakers”

Not much has changed since 1992 except maybe this quote is even more relevant.

We see it all around the information environment.

ISIS online recruiting; Russian propaganda; Chinese political warfare.

Information is its own warfare domain and our competitors and adversaries are waging battle there every minute of every day.

We are very good at influencing and controlling two of the three dimensions of the information domain:

the physical -- networks, Command and Control facilities, communication towers, phones, computers and such;

and the informational -- the collecting, processing, storing, disseminating and protecting of information.

But we’re not very good at influencing and controlling the third dimension of the information domain:

the cognitive dimension -- how people process and perceive information, make judgments, and make decisions on what to think and what to believe.

We lag in this dimension partly because it’s not in our culture and partly because our narrative lacks focus.

We're not going to change the culture part; nor should we.

Since 1791 and the First Amendment of the Bill of Rights, we’ve enjoyed Freedom of Speech and Freedom of the Press guarantees.

Where our adversaries shut down legitimate news and press coverage, or intimidate, silence, or even kill dissidents, we believe our country is stronger with a free press and with freedom of speech.

We can however, focus our narrative.

It is imperative for us to communicate the overarching narrative of the United States of America and link everything we do and everything we say to that narrative.

To do that, I believe we have three communication obligations:

One, to be transparent about our operations; and deliver Operational Truth.

Two, to ensure what we say aligns with what we do; and build Transactional Trust.

and three, to demonstrate who we are, as Sailors and Americans; by Reinforcing our Values in all our communication.

- - -

So what do I mean by “Operational Truth”?

It means aggressive transparency.

We must be able to tell and show the world what we are doing and why.

We must be proactive in our communication planning so we are able to get operational truth out into the information environment first and deny our competitors and adversaries informational maneuvering space.

Being first matters.

A RAND study on the Russian propaganda model says Russia has the agility to be first and that gives them the chance to create a first impression and follow it up with continuous multi-channel messaging which helps them boost their perceived credibility, expertise and trustworthiness.

- - -

On Screen: “Russian propaganda has the agility to be first, which affords propagandists the opportunity to create the first impression. Then, the combination of high-volume, multichannel, and continuous messaging makes Russian themes more likely to be familiar to their audiences, which gives them a boost in terms of perceived credibility, expertise, and trustworthiness.”

To combat this, we need to immediately be able to tell and show our part of the National narrative when asked.

This means we have an obligation to make publicly releasable information available to national leadership to provide context, facilitate informed perceptions, and counter adversarial propaganda.

Our units must be able to collect, process and forward for release, traditional VI like handheld imagery from specialized units like SNOOPIE, COMCAM and NPASE, or non-traditional VI from sensors on manned, unmanned and remotely piloted platforms or other appropriate data from GPS to radar tracks.

We have to view this information as a non-kinetic payload and empower our communication team -- IO and PA – to look at every exercise, operation or expected encounter through this lens.

We also need to consider when it’s appropriate to be more aggressive with releasing information about upcoming exercises and operations.

U.S. SIXTH FLEET did this in June when USS Porter was sent into the Black Sea. They announced on June 4th the intent to send Porter into the Black Sea.

On June 6th they announced Porter had entered and then on the 18th, that Porter had departed.

Partly because of these releases, Russia’s informational maneuver space was limited. It’s a smart use of communication strategy and aggressive transparency.

It also helps us build transactional trust.

- - -

Which brings me to our second communication obligation:

We need to ensure what we say aligns with what we do. In doing so, we will build transactional trust.

This is trust built over time by being transparent and closing transactional loops – big or small.

We see it all the time with the peer-to-peer economy. Yelp!, Airbnb, Uber.

They’re the new model of peer trust and they’re built on decentralized, bottom-up, personal reviews and online reputations. Transactional Trust.

People believe crowd-sourced reviews on Yelp!. They care about their Airbnb guest reputation and Uber passenger rating.

Trust in this context is not linear nor is it controlled by a single source. It works up and down; between provider and customer.

This type of peer trust is growing and transparency is a major reason why. Doing what you say you’re going to do builds transactional trust.

At a fundamental level, our Navy’s own transparent transactions may be why Gallup surveys for more than 20 years show high levels of public trust and confidence for our service.

Our role is to deploy where needed, remain ready to fight and win wars, and protect America.

Through time, there have been clearly visible transactions that have built our reputation:

At-sea battles during World War Two The rescue of Captain Phillips The raid that brought down Osama bin Laden Missile strikes from the sea to protect our Country.

We demonstrate our ability to protect America through each transaction.

But even as powerful as those events are, they wouldn’t be able to help the Navy build transactional trust without some level of transparency so people could see those actions, understand their context and make their own decision on whether the Navy is meeting their expectations.

This is why we communicate with our publics. We have to close the transactional loop to continue to build trust and understanding over time.

The PR firm Edelman conducts a worldwide trust survey each year. Their 2016 report – researched in October of 2015 -- divides the world into two groups:

The Informed Public -- college-educated, top 25 percent income earners with significant media consumption habits;

and then everyone else – the mass public, 85% of the world population.

Edelman’s research shows a growing gap between how these two groups view institutions.

The Informed Public reports 62 percent trust in government, media, business and NGOs, while the mass population reports less than 50 percent trust in those institutions.

And these surveys were taken a year ago. Before BREXIT. Before the Presidential Primaries and before the General Election season.

It’ll be interesting to see how those events shape trust levels.

From my seat, it’s alarming to see that 85 percent of the world’s population distrusts these mainstay institutions.

This is the audience our adversaries are targeting in their information war.

And it’s not just ‘over there’ in the Middle East, Russia or China – they’re also targeting us --- our mass population -- right here, right now.

There are two effective ways of combating adversarial propaganda and cyber recruiting.

One is continuing to close our ‘say-do’ gap and build and expand our transactional trust relationships.

The other is providing an alternative to the narrative our competitors and adversaries peddle online.

- - -

We do that by highlighting who we are and what we believe; By reinforcing our values through our actions and our stories.

Our American Dream is still a beacon of light and hope throughout the world.

Our Navy is a shining example of opportunity and progress, collaboration and acceptance, of being an important part of something bigger than yourself.

We value Toughness, Honesty, Initiative, and Innovation and demand Honor, Courage and Commitment.

And because of that, our Sailors grow to become leaders, experts in their fields, and designers of their own future.

Our Sailors represent the best in our country, Our ideals, hopes and aspirations.

We need to tell that story. It’ll help us combat our adversaries propaganda and value-based stories will help us build transactional trust.

- - -

Addressing all three dimensions of the information domain is vital for our ability to Project Power Ashore through information and communication.

We are very good at influencing the physical and informational dimensions, but face challenges working within the cognitive dimension.

And to change that, we must:

  • Focus our narrative
  • Deliver Operational Truth
  • Build Transactional Trust
  • And, Reinforce our Values

Thank you.
